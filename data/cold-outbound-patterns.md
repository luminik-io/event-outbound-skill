# Cold-Outbound Distilled Patterns

This document is a project reference for building the event-outbound skill, distilling recurring pattern shapes used in high-quality B2B cold outbound. The generated skill output never cites this document. All content is abstracted to pattern shapes and descriptions, not verbatim copies.

## Openers

Strong cold openers use relatable anecdotes, analogies, or provocative statements to draw the reader in without being overtly salesy. These often set up a contrast or introduce a counter-intuitive idea.

### Example Shapes:

*   **Relatable Analogy:** Starting with a story or comparison from an unexpected domain (e.g., iPhone, dire wolf, golf, cat bed) to illustrate a sales principle.
*   **Counter-Intuitive Claim:** Challenging common wisdom or sales advice to grab attention (e.g., "You don’t win in golf by focusing on winning.", "I don’t believe in sales stages.")
*   **Personal Anecdote:** Sharing a brief personal experience to build rapport and set context (e.g., "The other day, I was complaining to myself...").
*   **Perspective Shift:** Presenting a familiar situation from a new angle to highlight a hidden value or problem (e.g., LinkedIn post views).

## Objection Handlers / Framing

Strong outbound pre-empts objections and frames the conversation from the buyer’s perspective, often by "poking the bear" to uncover underlying issues rather than directly countering.

### Example Shapes:

*   **Customer-Centric Framing:** Acknowledging that the product/service isn’t for everyone, focusing on ideal buyers.
*   **Problem Illumination (Poke the Bear):** Asking questions that subtly highlight a potential problem with the prospect’s status quo, without being prescriptive or pushy (e.g., "How’s that been working out for you?" for a competitor, or the Mario Kart parking analogy).
*   **Shifting Focus from Outcome to Process:** Detaching from the immediate sales outcome to foster genuine curiosity and understanding.
*   **Labeling Emotions/Concerns:** Articulating the prospect’s potential unspoken hesitations (e.g., "Feels like you have some concerns.") to encourage openness.
*   **Pre-emptive Qualification:** Clearly stating potential reasons for misalignment upfront to filter out bad fits respectfully (e.g., the "4 reasons why I might not be a fit" email).

## Mid-Sequence Nudges / Follow-ups

Nudges are designed to be low-pressure, specific, and build on previous interactions or insights, rather than generic status-poke messages.

### Example Shapes:

*   **Value-Add Nudge:** Providing a new piece of relevant information or insight without demanding a response.
*   **Reference to Shared Context:** Referencing a previous interaction, event, or shared experience to maintain relevance.
*   **Low-Commitment Question:** Asking a simple, open-ended question that invites a brief response but doesn’t require deep engagement.
*   **Direct useful next step:** Attach or link the useful thing, then ask one real question about whether it maps to the recipient's work.

## Buyer-Controlled Selling Phrases

This approach keeps the prospect in control without adding fake friction. If a useful asset is part of the touch, include it directly instead of asking permission to send it.

### Example Shapes:

*   **"Not sure if you’ve run into this..."**: Introducing a common problem as a shared observation, inviting the prospect to confirm or deny its relevance to them.
*   **"You’re probably familiar with..."**: Presupposing some knowledge on the prospect’s part, making the interaction feel less like a pitch and more like a peer conversation.
*   **"I included the closest-lot link below."**: A low-friction offer that provides immediate value without asking for a meeting.
*   **"First, I wanted to find out if you’re okay with the $, terms, and dates."**: Explicitly asking for alignment on key deal parameters upfront to ensure mutual fit before deeper engagement.

## What Prompted the Outreach Lines

These lines clearly and concisely state the specific, concrete reason for the outreach, making it feel targeted and relevant to the recipient.

### Example Shapes:

*   **Event-Specific Reference:** "Looks like you’re catching Khrunben in Miami on the 23rd." Directly ties the outreach to a verifiable event or activity of the prospect.
*   **Activity-Based Reference:** Mentioning a recent LinkedIn post, comment, or article the prospect engaged with.

## Tonal Rules to Avoid

Strong outbound uses a direct, humble, and value-focused tone, actively avoiding common salesy clichés and high-pressure language.

### Example Shapes:

*   **No Pushy Language:** Avoid phrases that push, convince, or beg; keep a relaxed, curious approach.
*   **Absence of Superlatives/Hype:** Skip exaggerated language. See `data/cold-email-benchmarks.json` `buzzwords_banned` for the canonical list.
*   **Zero Emoji/Exclamation Marks:** Professional, understated tone — let substance carry the message.
*   **Avoiding Salesy Fillers:** Skip the standard empty-phrase set. The canonical blocklist lives in `data/cold-email-benchmarks.json` under `banned_words_and_phrases`.

## CTAs / Closes

Call-to-actions are clear, low-friction, and often offer value or probe for interest rather than immediately asking for a meeting.

### Example Shapes:

*   **Value-First Offer:** Proposing something useful to the prospect even if they don’t reply (e.g., sending a link).
*   **Probe for Interest/Problem:** Asking a question that invites the prospect to articulate their situation or interest rather than committing to a meeting (e.g., "What helps you come back to the moment?", "What role do you think confidence plays in sales?", "How are you handling parking when you go to events in Miami?" followed by listening).
*   **Explicit Invitation to Engage:** A clear, direct invitation to take a specific, low-commitment next step (e.g., "Your turn: [link]").

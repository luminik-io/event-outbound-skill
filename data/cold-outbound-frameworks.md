# Cold-Outbound Sales Frameworks

Internal reference. This document encodes generic cold-outbound craft principles as validation-rule shapes. No content here is redistributed in generated skill output; all shapes are abstracted patterns, not verbatim copy.

## 1. 4T Framework (Trigger, Think, Third-party Validation, Talk)
### Principle
The 4T framework helps construct cold emails that cut through resistance by focusing on the prospect's world, problems they might not know about, and social proof, leading to a permission-based call to action. It avoids direct pitching and hyperbole.

### Example Shapes
*   **Trigger**: "Noticed you're hiring SDRs..."
*   **Think (Illumination Question)**: "...which suggests you might be sending cold emails. How are you ensuring cold emails don't land in spam?"
*   **Third-Party Validation**: "Google and Salesforce are using us to deliver 94% of cold emails to inboxes compared to 12% before. It involves a warm-up tool that raises your inbox reputation to boost deliverability."
*   **Talk (Permission-Based CTA)**: "Worth a conversation?"

## 2. Poke the Bear
### Principle
Instead of directly pitching a solution, "poke the bear" by shining a light on a problem the prospect might not be aware of or fully understand. This creates curiosity and encourages them to seek more information from you. The question should be neutral and focus on their potential pain.

### Example Shapes
*   **Illumination Question**: "How are you ensuring your wash mitt won’t scratch your car?"
*   **Illumination Question**: "How are you reducing the risk of plantar fasciitis caused by 10-mile runs?"
*   **Illumination Question**: "How are you ensuring you’re not paying for subscriptions you forgot about?"

## 3. Cold Email Construction (Personal vs. Personalized)
### Principle
Effective cold emails start with a relevant opening, either personalized (specific to the individual) or personal (relevant to their situation). The goal is to set context, ask a "think" question that highlights an unknown problem, provide third-party validation, and offer a permission-based call to action. Avoid vague marketing language and focus on customer-centric problems.

### Example Shapes
*   **Personal (Situation-based)**: "Steve, end of the month and lots of commissions to pay."
*   **Think (Problem-focused)**: "Curious, how are you dealing with the labor-intensive, error-prone process of hard pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets running commissions?"
*   **Third-Party Validation (Contrast)**: "[Peer company] is using [tool] to run monthly commissions in five minutes instead of five hours. No rebuilding, no terrible things that you're doing today."
*   **CTA (Permission)**: "Worth the conversation?"

## 4. Subject Lines
### Principle
Subject lines should be short, intriguing, and prioritize the reader's concerns over the seller's pitch. They should create an "open loop" or highlight a problem without giving everything away, prompting the recipient to open the email. Referrals and personal mentions are highly effective.

### Example Shapes
*   **Referral**: "via Amanda" or "Bob sent me"
*   **Open Loop**: "Open to this?" or "Seen this?"
*   **Personal/Relevant**: "Pancakes" (if tied to their hobby) or "Comp at [Company Name]" (Job to be done)
*   **Problem-focused**: "arthritis?" or "low cold email response rates?"

## 5. Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework for Interviews
### Principle
The JTBD framework helps understand the true motivations and anxieties behind a customer's decision to "hire" a product. By reconstructing the timeline of their decision—from the initial thought to using the solution—you uncover their "buying language" and the "forces of progress" (pushes, pulls, habits, anxieties) that drive their choices.

### Example Shapes
*   **Warm-Up**: "I’m not here to sell you anything. I just want to understand the story of how you made your decision."
*   **Timeline Questions**: "When did you first think you might need something different?", "What changed that made this urgent?", "What worried you before deciding?"
*   **Digging Deeper**: "Tell me more," "What do you mean by that," "Can you give me an example?"
*   **Forces to Note**: Pushes of the current situation, pulls of the new solution, habits of the present, anxieties of the new.

## 6. Getting Replies
### Principle
To get replies, cold emails must address three questions the prospect is secretly asking: "Should I pay attention?", "Is this interesting?", and "Should I respond?". The content should shine a light on a significant, expensive problem that happens if they do nothing, creating demand rather than just pitching a better version of something they already have.

### Example Shapes
*   **Subject/First Line (Intriguing)**: Use open loops or direct relevance to their priorities.
*   **Problem-focused Content**: Frame your offering as solving a "big expensive terrible no good very bad thing" they might experience.
*   **Permission-Based CTA**: Ask for explicit interest, don't assume a meeting.

---

## 7. Asking Questions to Get Replies
### Principle
When asking questions, avoid leading questions that are in your best interest. Instead, ask "illumination questions" that shine a light on problems the prospect might not know about, or open-ended, multiple-choice questions that genuinely seek to understand their current process. This subtle approach fosters curiosity and encourages honest responses.

### Example Shapes
*   **Illumination Question (Neutral)**: "How are you ensuring your wash mitt won't scratch your car?"
*   **Multiple-Choice Question**: "How are you recovering failed payments? Are you using like an automation, do you have folks reaching out for you, or are you using an outsourced recovery team?"
*   **Gentle Curiosity**: "I'm just curious how are you doing this today? Is it like automated, you know, you got people reaching out or do you have like an outsourced team doing this?"

## 8. Cold Email Messages (Tonal Rules)
### Principle
The tone of cold email messages should be buyer-centric, specific, and avoid pushy sales language, hype, or generic buzzwords. Focus on demonstrating understanding of the prospect's world and offer concrete value or explicit interest, rather than defaulting to a meeting request.

### Example Shapes
*   **Avoid**: the standard salesy filler set (see `data/cold-email-benchmarks.json` banlist for the canonical list).
*   **Avoid**: Superlatives, hype, emoji, exclamation marks, and buzzwords (see `data/cold-email-benchmarks.json` `buzzwords_banned`).
*   **Focus**: Buyer-centric pronouns (you/your), super-specific references (role, company, event), clear CTAs (make offer +28% reply rate, ask for interest +7%).
